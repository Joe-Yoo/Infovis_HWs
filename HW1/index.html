Overview and Learning Objectives

For one day per week (Monday), we will focus on visualization research. We will read research papers on some topic/theme, students will make presentations about the papers, and we will discuss. One goal is to try to develop ideas for new research projects. On the other day per week (Wednesday), we will focus on visualization system/application development. Students will learn how to develop interactive visualization tools using languages and libraries like D3js, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

This course is an advanced information visualization course focusing on research within this domain. Students should already have a good working knowledge of data visualization along the lines of what one would get from taking CS 4460 or CS 6730 at Georgia Tech or equivalent courses. This course (CS 7450) is NOT intended to be a student's first exposure to the data visualization area.

Textbooks

There are no required textbooks for this course. However, there is a collection of resources that you should find helpful for assignments throughout the course. There are also readings that we will cover throughout the course, which are all available while on the GT VPN (or on campus).

Course Format

The course will consist mainly of two parts: reading and discussing research papers about information visualization and learning how to program web-based visualizations (predominantly using d3.js).

AI Assistant Policy

We treat AI-based assistance, such as ChatGPT and Copilot, the same way we treat collaboration with other people: you are welcome to talk about your ideas and work with other people, both inside and outside the class, as well as with AI-based assistants. However, all work you submit must be your own. You should never include in your assignment anything that was not written directly by you without proper citation (including quotation marks and in-line citation for direct quotes). Including anything you did not write in your assignment without proper citation will be treated as an academic misconduct case. If you are unsure where the line is between collaborating with AI and copying AI, we recommend the following heuristics:

  • Heuristic 1: Never hit "Copy" within your conversation with an AI assistant. You can copy your own work into your own conversation, but do not copy anything from the conversation back into your assignment. Instead, use your interaction with the AI assistant as a learning experience, then let your assignment reflect your improved understanding.
  • Heuristic 2: Do not have your assignment and the AI agent open at the same time. Similar to the above, use your conversation with the AI as a learning experience, then close the interaction down, open your assignment, and let your assignment reflect your revised knowledge. This heuristic includes avoiding using AI directly integrated into your composition environment: just as you should not let a classmate write content or code directly into your submission, so also you should avoid using tools that directly add content to your submission.

Deviating from these heuristics does not automatically qualify as academic misconduct; however, following these heuristics essentially guarantees your collaboration will not cross the line into misconduct.

Reading Reflections - 10%

Overview

Throughout the semester, you will complete three reading reflections. These reflections aim to deepen your understanding of visualization research papers and serve as examples for writing your own research proposal.

Each reflection has a different focus, aligning with the various parts of your research proposal (parts 1-3). Here are some general guidelines for all reflections:

  • Don't just repeat the paper.
  • Consider the pattern of summary -> interpret -> reflect for the key points of the paper. That means that you should identify one or more key points made in the paper, interpret what these mean, and then reflect more broadly on them.
  • Useful to end your reflection document with open-ended research questions, open discussion, etc.

You may choose to write a reflection on the paper you present or another one; the choice is yours.

Reading Reflection - 1 - Due: 1/30/2026 by 11:59 PM EST

This assignment focuses on the research idea of the paper. Consider the following:

  • What did the authors do?
  • How did the authors motivate this work? For example, how is it done today, and what are the limitations of current practices?
  • What was the authors' approach, and how did they convince others of its success?

Remember, do not simply repeat the content. Use the summary -> interpret -> reflect pattern. Discuss what the authors did well and what could be improved.

Consider this process as a way to learn how to write your Prototype Part 1 assignment. Taking the paper you read as an example.

Which paper to read: Choose one paper from the entire reading list. The chosen paper should be different from the papers you use in the other two reading reflections.

What to submit: Submit a PDF of your reflection, 1-2 pages, single-spaced, single-column, with a reasonable font size (~12pt).

Reading Reflection - 2 - Due: 2/20/2026 by 11:59 PM EST

This individual assignment focuses on the literature review section of the paper. While you should briefly describe the research idea, there's no need for extensive detail. Your reflection should include:

  • Research Idea: What was the research idea, and why is it novel and significant? (Briefly)
  • Research Idea:
    • What existing work is related to the research idea?
    • What different research directions or sub-areas are described by the authors? Are these comprehensive? Why might another research direction (e.g., X) not be included?
    • What are the representative works closely related to this research? What did they accomplish, and how do they relate to this research idea? Why there is still the need to carry out this work in such contexts?

Remember, do not simply repeat the content. Use the summary -> interpret -> reflect pattern. Discuss what the authors did well and what could be improved.

Consider this process as a way to learn how to write your Prototype Part 2 assignment. Taking the paper you read as an example.

Which paper to read: Choose one paper from the entire reading list. The chosen paper should be different from the papers you use in the other two reading reflections.

What to submit: Submit a PDF of your reflection, 1-2 pages, single-spaced, single-column, with a reasonable font size (~12pt).

Research Prototype - 35%

Overview

The goal of this assignment group (research prototype) is to give you an end-to-end experience of a visualization research project.

You will write a complete proposal for a potential new research project in information visualization, and produce a preliminary research prototype.

Largely speaking, there are two major parts involved in almost any research project: Ideation (parts 1-2) and Implementation (parts 3-5).

To complete this proposal, follow these steps:

  1. Initial Ideation & Literature Review: Develop an initial (broad) research project idea with a clear goal, motivation, and contribution. Then conduct a thorough literature review to highlight the novelty and significance of your topic compared to existing work.
  2. Polished Ideation: Narrow down the scope of your research project idea based on the literature review. Make it clear about its novelty and significance of your proposed work.
  3. Method and Plan: Create a comprehensive research plan, detailing the methods and setup for your project.
  4. Prototype: Implement the research plan.

By completing these steps, you'll have an almost-ready project that could lead to a conference submission, such as the IEEE VIS Short Paper track. You could also use it for an independent study or a PhD project in visualization. Of course, continuing with your proposed project is up to you.

Individual or Team: You can choose to complete the research prototype individually or as a team (with maximum of 3 students).

Please sign up your team using the spreadsheet: CS7450-Spring26-PrototypeTeamSignUp.xlsx.

If you decide to work as a team, in the first submission, a "team agreement" needs to be submitted, including details about how and when to collaborate. For example, the answers to the following questions:

  • Who is the team leader?
  • How will you communicate?
  • How/when will you meet?
  • How will you collaborate on implementation?
  • How will you deal with non-performing members?

Only the team leader needs to submit for all prototype assignment submissions.

Prototype Part 1 - Initial Ideation & Literature Review - Due: 2/13/2026 by 11:59 PM EST - 5%

For Part 1, you need to write the first draft of "introduction" and "related work" sections of a research paper to describe your research idea, and what is the current state-of-the-art.

Start by considering how to generate a strong research idea with a scope achievable in 1-2 semesters. Aim for proposals that are neither too simple nor overly complex.

A good place to start for this is to think about which "type" of research (and thus proposal) you want to focus on.

  • New Technique: Identify a gap for a new visualization or interaction technique. Describe this gap and how your work will address it, including design goals and sketches.
  • New User Study: Identify a knowledge gap in visualization that needs a well-designed study. Outline the gap, research questions, and methodology.
  • New Domain Tool: Consider a domain where visualization could enhance workflow. Describe the existing workflow and how visualization can help.

The readings in the first three topics focus on one of these types respectively. There are other "types," but these above should get you started. If you are interested in other types, take a look at the Six VIS Areas defined by the IEEE VIS paper submission process. Here is another magazine-styled article explaining various visualization research contributions.

After identify a broad research idea, it's essential to conduct a thorough literature review of existing work related to your research idea. This helps ensure your idea remains novel and that you're not reinventing the wheel.

Steps for Conducting a Literature Review:

  1. Identify 2-3 relevant sub-directions. It is always a good idea to divide and conquer, which also makes your literature review more structured.
  2. Search for papers. Considering the following tips:
    • Prioritize papers from reputable venues: A Guide to the Quality of Different Visualization Venues.
    • Use the "source" filter from Google Scholar, e.g., limit papers from TVCG.
  3. Read the searched papers.
    • Before reading the full paper, do a quick skim (e.g., reading the abstract and figures) to make sure it is worthy reading.
  4. Summarize them in a good narrative.

You should read at least 10 relevant papers and include the discussion of them in your report.

Evaluation: Your proposal will be graded on clarity, thoughtfulness, and articulation.

Writing Tip: You are also expected to submit a "Reading Reflection" at the same time in a separate assignment. Using your selected paper as an example. Write a similar "introduction" and "related work" for this assignment.

Submission Guidelines: Submit a 1-2 page document in double-column conference format (title required, no abstract needed) as a PDF. Use the following template for formatting: IEEE Template.

Please also include your team agreement as a separate page that DOES NOT need to follow the template.

Prototype Part 2 - Polished Ideation & Expanded Literature Review - Due: 2/27/2026 by 11:59 PM EST - 5%

Oftentimes, after an initial literature review round, researchers want to polish their proposed research idea. Researchers now have a more comprehensive understanding of what has been done in the direction.

The goal of this part is to make your research idea more specific and concrete, usually with a narrower scope to highlight its novelty. Another goal is to obtain a solid understanding of the significance of your proposed project, and articulate it well.

What to do: First, expand your literature review, including at least 20 papers in total now, and discuss them in your "related work" section now. Then, revise your "introduction" section to make it crystal-clear about the following questions:

  1. What are you trying to do? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.
  2. How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?
  3. What is new in your approach and why do you think it will be successful?
  4. Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?

Making sure, the novelty and significance of your proposed idea is clearly articulated and convincing.

Writing Tip: take your research reflection assignment as a chance to do a deep analysis of a modal paper.

Submission Guidelines: We want you to submit two things:

  1. IEEE format report. Update the content and submit it.
  2. A 2-min video explaining your ideas, covering the answers to the four questions aforementioned.

Zip these two files, and submit them together.
